# Comparative Advantage

**School of Thought:** Classical Economics

> **Attribution note:** Comparative Advantage was formalized by **David Ricardo** (1817) in *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, not by Adam Smith. It is a direct extension and correction of Smith's earlier theory of [Absolute Advantage](absolute-advantage.md). This page is included because it is referenced throughout the wiki wherever trade theory is discussed.

## Core Claims and Assumptions
Even if one country can produce *every* good more efficiently than another (i.e., holds an absolute advantage in everything), both countries still benefit from trade — provided each specialises in the good where its *relative* (opportunity) cost is lowest.

- **Core claim:** A country should export goods for which it sacrifices the least alternative production, and import goods for which the opportunity cost of domestic production is highest.
- **Key mechanism:** Opportunity cost, not absolute productivity, determines the pattern of beneficial trade.
- **Assumptions:** Two countries, two goods (in the simplest model); constant returns to scale; perfect mobility of labour within countries but immobility across borders; no transport costs or trade barriers.

## Predictive Power and Empirical Record
Strong for explaining the *direction* of specialisation in historical trade patterns (e.g., 19th-century Britain exporting manufactured goods, importing agricultural commodities). Modern extensions (Heckscher–Ohlin, New Trade Theory) address the model's failure to account for economies of scale, product differentiation, and intra-industry trade.

## Criticisms from Competing Schools
- **Absolute Advantage baseline:** Smith's [Absolute Advantage](absolute-advantage.md) already captured obvious cases; Ricardo's contribution was showing trade is beneficial even in non-obvious cases where one partner is less efficient at everything.
- **Infant industry / strategic trade:** Protectionist critics (e.g., Friedrich List) argue comparative advantage is a static concept — a country locked into low-value exports today may never develop high-value industries. State intervention can shift a country's comparative advantage over time.
- **Distributional effects:** Even if comparative advantage produces aggregate gains from trade, those gains are unevenly distributed within countries, generating losers alongside winners (Stolper–Samuelson theorem).
- **Factor mobility:** The assumption that labour cannot move across borders is increasingly questionable in a world of migration and global capital flows.

## Current Status
**Mainstream and foundational.** Comparative advantage remains the central organising principle of international trade theory in mainstream economics, though it is increasingly supplemented by New Trade Theory models that allow for economies of scale and imperfect competition.
