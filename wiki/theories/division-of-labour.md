# Division of Labour

**School of Thought:** [Classical Economics](../economists/adam-smith.md)

## Core Claims and Assumptions
The division of labour is the specialization of cooperative labour in specific, circumscribed tasks and roles.
- **Claims:** Productivity increases significantly when tasks are broken down into smaller parts. This is due to (1) increased dexterity in every particular workman, (2) saving of time which is commonly lost in passing from one species of work to another, and (3) the invention of a great number of machines which facilitate and abridge labour.
- **Assumptions:** Assumes a market large enough to support specialization ("the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market") and the existence of trade to exchange specialized products.

## Predictive Power and Empirical Record
Extremely high. The division of labour is a fundamental principle of modern industrial organization, manufacturing, and the digital economy (e.g., software engineering roles, specialized services). Smith's pin factory example remains the classic empirical illustration.

## Criticisms from Competing Schools
- **Marxist Critique:** Karl Marx argued that extreme division of labour leads to "alienation" of the worker, as they perform repetitive, meaningless tasks and lose connection to the final product.
- **Humanistic/Psychological:** Critics argue it can lead to mental degradation and "intellectual stagnation" (a point Smith himself acknowledged in *Wealth of Nations*).

## Current Status
**Mainstream**. It remains a core concept in modern economics, management, and global trade (International Division of Labour).
