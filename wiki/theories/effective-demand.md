# Effective Demand

**School of Thought:** Keynesian Economics  
**Core Assumptions:** Aggregate supply and aggregate demand are not automatically equal at full employment; price and wage stickiness may prevent market clearing.

## Core Claims
Effective demand is the total amount of spending on goods and services in an economy. It is the point where the aggregate demand function (what firms expect to sell) intersects with the aggregate supply function (the cost of production).
- The level of employment depends on the volume of effective demand.
- If effective demand is insufficient, firms will not hire workers, leading to unemployment.
- Unlike classical theory, which assumes "supply creates its own demand" (Say's Law), Keynesian theory posits that demand creates its own supply (up to the point of full capacity).

## Predictive Power and Empirical Record
- Successfully explained the persistence of the Great Depression, which classical theory could not account for.
- Forms the basis of modern demand management policies (fiscal and monetary stimulus).

## Criticisms from Competing Schools
- **Classical/Supply-Side:** Argue that focusing only on demand ignores the incentives for production and investment.
- **Austrian School:** Contend that artificial boosting of demand leads to malinvestment and unsustainable booms.

## Current Status
- **Mainstream:** Highly influential in macroeconomics, particularly in New Keynesian models.
