# Absolute Advantage

**School of Thought:** [Classical Economics](../economists/adam-smith.md)

## Core Claims and Assumptions
A country has an absolute advantage in producing a good if it can produce it more efficiently than another country (i.e., using fewer resources or less labour).
- **Claims:** Each nation should specialize in the products it is best at and trade them for other goods. This increases the total global output and benefit for all involved.
- **Assumptions:** Assumes there are no trade barriers (tariffs/quotas) and low transportation costs. Focuses on production efficiency alone.

## Predictive Power and Empirical Record
High for explaining basic patterns of international trade. It was a groundbreaking departure from mercantilist ideas of zero-sum trade.

## Criticisms from Competing Schools
- **Comparative Advantage (Ricardo):** David Ricardo argued that even if a country has an absolute advantage in producing *everything*, trade is still beneficial as long as it specializes in goods where it has the largest relative advantage (lower opportunity cost).
- **Protectionism:** Critics argue that absolute advantage ignores the importance of developing new industries or protecting vital national interests (infant industry argument).

## Current Status
**Mainstream**. It forms the foundational basis for understanding trade, though it was later refined and expanded by [Comparative Advantage](comparative-advantage.md).
