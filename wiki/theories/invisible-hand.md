# Invisible Hand

**School of Thought:** [Classical Economics](../economists/adam-smith.md)

## Core Claims and Assumptions
The "invisible hand" is a metaphor describing how individuals, by pursuing their own self-interest in a competitive market, are led by an invisible hand to promote the interests of society, even when that was not their intention.
- **Claims:** Market competition acts as a self-regulating mechanism that channels private gain into public benefit (lower prices, better products). It guides resource allocation toward where it is most valued.
- **Assumptions:** Competitive markets with low entry/exit barriers, property rights, and participants who act rationally in their own self-interest.

## Predictive Power and Empirical Record
Mixed. While markets are highly efficient at distributing goods and services and signaling scarcity through prices, the model's pure form is often challenged.

## Criticisms from Competing Schools
- **Keynesian/Institutional:** Emphasizes market failures, externalities (pollution), and the tendency for monopolies to form. Information asymmetry (not everyone has the same information) also undermines the hand's efficiency.
- **Behavioral Economics:** Challenges the assumption of rational self-interest.
- **Ecological Economics:** Criticizes its failure to account for natural resource depletion and environmental costs.

## Current Status
**Mainstream but qualified.** Most economists recognize the power of market mechanisms but acknowledge the need for regulation to address market failures.
