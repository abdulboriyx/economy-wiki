# Keynesian Multiplier

**School of Thought:** Keynesian Economics  
**Core Assumptions:** Some portion of any increase in income will be spent on consumption (the Marginal Propensity to Consume).

## Core Claims
The multiplier is the ratio of the change in national income to the initial change in autonomous spending (like investment or government spending) that caused it.
- An initial injection of spending creates income for others, who then spend a portion of that income, creating more income, and so on.
- The size of the multiplier depends on the Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC). If people save more (lower MPC), the multiplier is smaller.
- Formula: $Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC)$

## Predictive Power and Empirical Record
- Used to justify government spending as a way to "prime the pump" during recessions.
- Empirical estimates of the multiplier vary widely, often depending on the state of the economy (higher during recessions).

## Criticisms from Competing Schools
- **Monetarists:** Argue that government spending "crowds out" private spending through higher interest rates.
- **Ricardian Equivalence:** Suggests that if people expect future taxes to pay for current spending, they will save their current income, negating the multiplier effect.

## Current Status
- **Mainstream:** A core concept in introductory macroeconomics and fiscal policy analysis.
