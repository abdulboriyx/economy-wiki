# Natural vs Market Price

## Definition
- **Natural Price:** The "central" price that covers the cost of production (wages, rent, and profit) at their ordinary rates. It is the price an item would have if production were in perfect balance with demand.
- **Market Price:** The actual price at which a good is sold at any given moment. It is determined by the quantity brought to market vs. the "effectual demand" (demand from those willing and able to pay the natural price).

## Distinguishing from related concepts
Market price is a short-term phenomenon, whereas natural price is a long-term "equilibrium" toward which the market price is constantly gravitating. This distinction is the core of the **Price Mechanism** model.

## Real-world examples
- **Surge Pricing:** When demand for rides spikes (e.g., during rain), the market price rises far above the natural price. Over time, more drivers enter the market (increasing supply), pushing the market price back down toward the natural price.
- **Agricultural Goods:** A bad harvest might drive market prices up for a season, but over years, the price tends to return to the natural price as long as the cost of production remains stable.
