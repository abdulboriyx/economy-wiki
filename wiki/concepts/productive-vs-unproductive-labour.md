# Productive vs Unproductive Labour

## Definition
- **Productive Labour:** Labour that adds to the exchangeable value of the good or service being produced, and that fixes or realises itself in some vendable commodity which endures after the labour is complete. A manufacturer's labour, for example, is productive because it is embodied in the finished good.
- **Unproductive Labour:** Labour that produces no vendable commodity and whose value perishes in the instant of its performance. The labour of a menial servant, an actor, or a government official falls into this category in Smith's framework — the service is consumed at the moment it is rendered.

## Distinguishing from Related Concepts
This distinction should not be confused with the [Division of Labour](../theories/division-of-labour.md): division of labour describes *how* productive work is organised; the productive/unproductive distinction describes *what kind of output* that work yields. A pin-factory worker is both a participant in a divided labour process *and* a productive labourer in Smith's sense.

## Smith's Examples
- **Productive:** Manufacturing workers (spinners, weavers, smiths) — their labour is stored in the commodity and sold later.
- **Unproductive:** Domestic servants, lawyers, physicians, men of letters, actors, musicians, and the sovereign and all civil and military officers — their services, however valuable socially, leave no lasting vendable product.

Smith's concern was macroeconomic: nations that direct too high a proportion of their annual revenue toward unproductive employments accumulate less capital and grow more slowly.

## Criticisms
- **Marxist reframing:** Marx adopted the productive/unproductive distinction but relocated its axis: for Marx, productive labour is labour that produces *surplus value* for a capitalist, not merely a physical commodity. A transport worker, though producing no tangible good, is productive in Marx's sense.
- **Modern service economy:** In a post-industrial economy where services constitute the majority of GDP, Smith's framework struggles. Financial analysts, software developers, and healthcare workers create enormous economic value but produce no "vendable commodity" in Smith's original sense.
- **Normative objection:** Critics argue Smith's categorisation carries an unwarranted moral or policy implication — that governments should tax or curtail "unproductive" spending — which does not follow straightforwardly from the definition.
