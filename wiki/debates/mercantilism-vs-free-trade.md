# Mercantilism vs Free Trade

## The Disagreement
This is one of the oldest and most consequential debates in economics: should the state actively manage trade to accumulate wealth, or should trade be left free to generate mutual gains through specialisation?

---

## School 1: Mercantilism
**Period:** Dominant European doctrine, roughly 16th–18th centuries (Colbert, Mun, Misselden)

**Core position:** National wealth consists primarily in the stock of gold and silver (precious metals) held by the sovereign. Trade is inherently zero-sum: one country's export surplus is another's loss.

**Policy prescriptions:**
- Maximise exports, minimise imports to generate a trade surplus.
- Use tariffs, quotas, and monopoly trading companies to protect domestic industries.
- Colonise territories to extract raw materials cheaply and sell manufactured goods back at a premium.
- Hoard bullion; restrict its export.

**Evidence cited:** The apparent success of trading empires (Spain, Portugal, later England and the Dutch Republic) that actively managed their trade balances and accumulated colonial wealth.

---

## School 2: Classical Free Trade (Smith and Ricardo)
**Period:** Late 18th century onward; [Adam Smith](../economists/adam-smith.md), David Ricardo

**Core position:** Wealth is not gold but the productive capacity of a nation — its ability to generate goods and services. Trade is *positive-sum*: both partners gain when each specialises in what it produces relatively most efficiently.

**Policy prescriptions:**
- Remove tariffs, quotas, and monopoly privileges.
- Let each country specialise according to [Absolute Advantage](../theories/absolute-advantage.md) (Smith) or [Comparative Advantage](comparative-advantage.md) (Ricardo).
- The [Invisible Hand](../theories/invisible-hand.md) coordinates production and exchange without central direction.
- Colonies are more burden than asset once their trade monopolies are dismantled.

**Evidence cited:** Smith's critique of the Navigation Acts and East India Company monopolies; Ricardo's formal proof that comparative advantage produces mutual gains even when one country is less efficient at everything.

---

## Comparing the Evidence

| Dimension | Mercantilism | Free Trade |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Nature of trade | Zero-sum | Positive-sum |
| Source of wealth | Bullion stocks | Productive capacity |
| Role of the state | Active manager | Minimal intervener |
| Colonial policy | Extract and control | Politically costly; trade freely |
| Empirical support | Short-run trade balances | Long-run growth and specialisation |

---

## Points of Genuine Disagreement
- **Infant industry:** Even committed free-traders concede that a country may need temporary protection to build comparative advantage in new industries (Mill, Hamilton). Mercantilists see this as validation; free-traders see it as a strictly bounded exception.
- **National security:** Strategic industries (defence, food) may justify protection even under free-trade frameworks. The line between security exception and mercantile rent-seeking is contested.
- **Distributional effects:** Free trade produces aggregate gains but can devastate specific industries and workers. Mercantilism, by protecting incumbents, avoided some of this disruption at the cost of overall efficiency.

> **Do not collapse this disagreement.** The debate remains live in contemporary politics (tariffs, industrial policy, "reshoring"). Mainstream economics leans heavily toward free trade on efficiency grounds; heterodox and nationalist schools preserve significant elements of mercantilist reasoning about strategic trade.

---

## Contemporary Relevance
The mercantilist/free-trade axis resurfaces in debates over:
- US–China trade relations and technology export controls
- EU industrial policy and strategic autonomy
- "Friend-shoring" and supply-chain resilience arguments post-COVID
