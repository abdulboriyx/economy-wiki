# Classical vs. Keynesian Economics

**Overview:** One of the most fundamental debates in economics, centered on whether the economy is self-correcting or requires government intervention to achieve full employment.

## Comparison of Sides

### Classical View
- **Core Assumption:** Say's Law — "Supply creates its own demand."
- **Equilibrium:** Always tends toward full employment; any unemployment is either frictional or voluntary.
- **Prices and Wages:** Perfectly flexible; they adjust quickly to clear markets.
- **Role of Savings:** Savings automatically become investment through the interest rate mechanism.
- **Policy:** Laissez-faire; government intervention causes more harm than good (e.g., through inflation or debt).

### Keynesian View
- **Core Assumption:** Effective Demand — "Demand creates its own supply."
- **Equilibrium:** Can exist at any level of employment, including high unemployment (under-employment equilibrium).
- **Prices and Wages:** "Sticky" in the downward direction; they do not adjust quickly to clear markets.
- **Role of Savings:** High savings can lead to lower aggregate demand (the "paradox of thrift") and deep recessions.
- **Policy:** Active management through fiscal stimulus (spending/tax cuts) and monetary policy.

## Evidence and Historical Context
- **Classical Success:** Explained much of the economic growth during the 19th-century Industrial Revolution.
- **Keynesian Success:** Provided the blueprint for recovery from the Great Depression and the post-WWII economic boom.
- **Keynesian Failure:** Struggled to explain "stagflation" (high inflation + high unemployment) in the 1970s.
- **Neoclassical Synthesis:** Modern macroeconomics often uses Keynesian models for the short run and Classical models for the long run.
