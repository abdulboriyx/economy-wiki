# Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply (AD–AS) Model

**What the model explains:** The determination of the price level and total output in an economy. It combines the Keynesian focus on demand with the classical focus on supply.

## Key Assumptions
- **Aggregate Demand (AD):** The total amount of goods and services demanded at different price levels. It slopes downward due to the wealth effect, the interest rate effect, and the exchange rate effect.
- **Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS):** The total amount of goods and services firms are willing to produce at different price levels. It slopes upward because of "sticky" wages and prices.
- **Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS):** Vertical at the potential level of output (full employment), representing the classical view that in the long run, output is determined by technology and resources, not the price level.

## Outputs and Predictions
- **Equilibrium:** Where AD = SRAS. This can be below, at, or above the full-employment level (LRAS).
- **Recessionary Gap:** When AD is too low, the economy is in equilibrium below full employment (involuntary unemployment).
- **Inflationary Gap:** When AD is too high, leading to upward pressure on prices.

## Known Failures or Limitations
- **Stagflation:** A simultaneous increase in inflation and unemployment (shift in SRAS) was difficult to explain with simple AD-side management.
- **Simplification:** Aggregates a complex economy into two simple curves, which may miss sectoral shifts.
