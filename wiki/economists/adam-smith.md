# Adam Smith

**Era:** 18th Century (Scottish Enlightenment)  
**School of Thought:** Classical Economics

## Core Contributions
Adam Smith is widely regarded as the father of modern economics. His core contributions include the theory of the [Division of Labour](../theories/division-of-labour.md), the conceptualization of the [Invisible Hand](../theories/invisible-hand.md), and the formulation of the theory of [Absolute Advantage](../theories/absolute-advantage.md) in international trade. He provided the first comprehensive critique of mercantilism, laying the intellectual groundwork for free-market capitalism.

## Key Works
- *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759)
- [*An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*](../sources/wealth-of-nations.md) (1776)

## Influence and Legacy
Smith's work established economics as an independent discipline. His ideas deeply influenced 19th-century classical economists (like David Ricardo and John Stuart Mill) and remain central to neoclassical economics and modern defenses of free trade and deregulation.

## Criticisms
Critics, particularly from Marxist and heterodox schools, argue that Smith's system overlooks class conflict and the exploitative nature of capitalist property relations. Furthermore, his reliance on the "invisible hand" is criticized for ignoring market failures, externalities, and the necessity of state intervention to ensure equitable outcomes. His [Productive vs Unproductive Labour](../concepts/productive-vs-unproductive-labour.md) distinction is largely rejected by modern economists.
