# The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money

**Author:** [John Maynard Keynes](../economists/john-maynard-keynes.md)  
**Year:** 1936  
**Source URL:** `raw/keynes.pdf`

## Overview
*The General Theory* is the foundational text of Keynesian economics. It challenged the prevailing classical orthodoxy which held that markets are self-correcting and that full employment is the natural equilibrium of a market economy. Keynes introduced the idea that aggregate demand determines the level of economic activity and that involuntary unemployment can persist indefinitely without government intervention.

## Core Argument
Keynes argues that the level of employment is determined by "effective demand"—the point where aggregate supply meets aggregate demand. Because individuals and firms may choose to hoard money (liquidity preference) rather than invest or consume, there is no guarantee that total spending will be sufficient to employ all willing workers. Therefore, the economy can get stuck in an "under-employment equilibrium."

## Key Claims

### Effective Demand Determines Employment
- **What is argued:** The level of employment depends on the total volume of spending in the economy.
- **Evidence or model used:** The intersection of the aggregate demand function and the aggregate supply function.
- **Strength:** Strong. This shifted the focus of economics from supply-side to demand-side analysis.

### The Multiplier Effect
- **What is argued:** An initial increase in investment or government spending leads to a more than proportional increase in total national income.
- **Evidence or model used:** The relationship between the marginal propensity to consume and the resulting rounds of spending.
- **Strength:** Strong. It provides the theoretical basis for fiscal stimulus.

### Liquidity Preference and Interest Rates
- **What is argued:** Interest rates are not the "price" of savings, but the reward for parting with liquidity. People hold money for transaction, precautionary, and speculative motives.
- **Evidence or model used:** Analysis of the psychological and business incentives to hold cash.
- **Strength:** Moderate to Strong. While foundational, modern central banking focuses more on inflation targeting than pure liquidity preference.

### Say's Law is Fallacious
- **What is argued:** Supply does NOT create its own demand. Income earned from production is not automatically spent on other products; it can be saved in a way that reduces aggregate demand.
- **Evidence or model used:** Critique of classical postulates and the "optical illusion" of the classical theory of interest.
- **Strength:** Contested. While dominant in the mid-20th century, new classical economists later sought to re-establish supply-side foundations.

## What this source adds
It provides the first comprehensive macro-economic framework that accounts for persistent unemployment and justifies the role of government in managing the economy through fiscal and monetary policy.

## Limitations and assumptions
Keynes focuses primarily on the short run ("In the long run we are all dead"). He assumes sticky wages and prices (at least in the downward direction) and relies heavily on psychological factors like "animal spirits."

## Open questions
- How can the theory be extended to account for long-run growth and inflation (stagflation)?
- To what extent are the psychological "propensities" stable over time?

## How this updates or contradicts existing wiki pages
It directly contradicts the [Invisible Hand](../theories/invisible-hand.md) and [Natural vs Market Price](../concepts/natural-vs-market-price.md) mechanisms described by Smith, arguing that the "natural" state of the market is not necessarily one of full employment.
