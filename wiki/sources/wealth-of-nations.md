# An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations

**Author:** [Adam Smith](../economists/adam-smith.md)  
**Year:** 1776  
**Source URL:** `raw/Wealth-Nations.pdf`

## Overview
*The Wealth of Nations* is a foundational text in classical economics that systematically describes the mechanisms of a free-market economy. It examines how nations accumulate wealth, primarily through the division of labor, the accumulation of capital, and the unhindered functioning of free markets.

## Core Argument
Smith argues that wealth is not measured by the accumulation of gold and silver (as mercantilists believed), but by the total production and commerce of a nation. He posits that individuals pursuing their own self-interest in a free market inadvertently contribute to the overall economic prosperity of society, a mechanism he famously termed the "invisible hand."

## Key Claims

### The Division of Labour Increases Productivity
- **What is argued:** Breaking down production into specialized tasks dramatically increases the productivity of labor.
- **Evidence or model used:** The famous example of the pin factory, where specialized workers produce vastly more pins than if each worker made whole pins independently.
- **Strength:** Strong. This remains a cornerstone of modern industrial organization.

### Free Trade is Superior to Mercantilism
- **What is argued:** Nations benefit from trading freely rather than accumulating precious metals through restrictive tariffs and monopolies. Countries should produce what they have an absolute advantage in and trade for the rest.
- **Evidence or model used:** Critique of European mercantile policies, colonial trade monopolies, and bounties.
- **Strength:** Strong. Forms the basis of classical and neoclassical trade theory.

### Markets are Self-Regulating (The Invisible Hand)
- **What is argued:** Through the price mechanism, supply and demand naturally equilibrate without the need for central planning.
- **Evidence or model used:** The distinction between [Natural vs Market Price](../concepts/natural-vs-market-price.md), showing how market prices gravitate toward natural prices (costs of production).
- **Strength:** Strong (conceptually), though contested in practice due to market failures.

## What this source adds
It represents the first comprehensive system of political economy, shifting the focus from sovereign wealth (mercantilism) to national income and the productivity of labor.

## Limitations and assumptions
Smith assumes highly competitive markets, perfect mobility of labor and capital, and underestimates the potential for systemic market failures or monopolies to arise naturally. His distinction between productive and unproductive labor is also heavily debated.

## Open questions
- To what extent can the "invisible hand" be relied upon without state intervention?
- Is the labor theory of value (which Smith partially espouses) an adequate explanation of relative prices?

## How this updates or contradicts existing wiki pages
This foundational text establishes the classical school, countering the mercantile doctrines that previously dominated European economic thought.
